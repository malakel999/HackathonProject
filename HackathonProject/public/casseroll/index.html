The Earth, positioned at 1 AU, is the only known planet to support life, with a diverse range of ecosystems and climates. The Earth-Moon barycenter, which represents the center of mass between the Earth and its Moon, significantly influences ocean tides. Its Keplerian elements include an eccentricity of 0.017 and an inclination of -0.000°.

Earth, Life

Jupiter, the largest planet in the solar system at 5.20 AU, is renowned for its Great Red Spot, a giant storm, and its numerous moons, including Ganymede, the largest moon in the solar system. With a thick atmosphere primarily made of hydrogen and helium, Jupiter has a strong magnetic field and extensive ring system. Its Keplerian elements show an eccentricity of 0.048 and an inclination of 1.30°.

Jupiter, Giant

Mars, situated at 1.52 AU, captivates with its desert landscapes and polar ice caps, making it a key target in the search for extraterrestrial life. Known as the "Red Planet," it has a thin atmosphere and features such as the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons. The Keplerian elements for Mars include an eccentricity of 0.093 and an inclination of 1.85°.

Mars, Water

Mercury, the closest planet to the Sun at 0.39 AU, is known for its extreme temperatures and cratered surface, resembling that of the Moon. It has a thin atmosphere and experiences drastic temperature fluctuations due to its proximity to the Sun. The Keplerian elements for Mercury include an eccentricity of 0.206 and an inclination of 7.00°.

Mercury, Heat

Neptune, positioned at 30.07 AU, is known for its intense storms and deep blue color, attributed to methane in its atmosphere. It possesses a dynamic atmosphere with strong winds and a set of faint rings. The Keplerian elements for Neptune show an eccentricity of 0.009 and an inclination of 1.77°.

Neptune, Wind

Pluto, classified as a dwarf planet, has a highly elliptical and inclined orbit, distinguishing it from other planets in the solar system. With an eccentricity of 0.2488, it moves significantly farther from the Sun at certain points in its orbit. Its orbital period of 248 years highlights its great distance from the Sun. As a member of the Kuiper Belt, Pluto reveals fascinating characteristics about the dynamics of celestial bodies located far from the Sun.

Pluto, Dwarf

Saturn, located at 9.54 AU, is famous for its stunning rings, composed of ice and rock particles. It has a diverse collection of moons, with Titan being the most significant due to its dense atmosphere and surface lakes of liquid methane. The Keplerian elements for Saturn include an eccentricity of 0.054 and an inclination of 2.49°.

Saturn, Rings

The Sun is the central body of the solar system, exerting gravitational influence over all celestial bodies, including planets, asteroids, and comets. As the primary source of light and heat, it is essential for life on Earth. Its stability and massive mass make it a key element in the dynamics of the solar system.

Sun, Energy

Uranus, at 19.19 AU, is unique for its extreme axial tilt, causing it to roll along its orbit. It has a faint ring system and several moons, with a blue-green color resulting from methane in its atmosphere. The Keplerian elements for Uranus include an eccentricity of 0.047 and an inclination of 0.77°.

Uranus, Tilted

Venus, located at 0.72 AU, is often referred to as Earth's "sister planet" because of its similar size. However, it is shrouded in a thick, toxic atmosphere primarily composed of carbon dioxide, resulting in intense greenhouse heating. The Keplerian elements for Venus show an eccentricity of 0.007 and an inclination of 3.39°.
